TIPS FOR CHOOSING SPECIALIZATION

Consider the following tips for choosing a civil engineering specialization at college:

Evaluate your skill sets: Assessing your technical capabilities and learning goals may help you narrow your options. For example, if you're skilled in chemical analysis, you might benefit from a career as a materials engineer.

Assess your interests. Reflect on your hobbies and value system to determine a career path that can lead to fulfillment. For instance, if you express an interest in activism, education and sustainability, you may enjoy an environmental engineering specialization.

Apply for an internship. Many companies and research centers offer internships to high school students and individuals with a GED, which may help you learn which most appeals to your interests. To find opportunities, you can connect with an academic adviser or research options online.

Consider job outlook and salary potential. Depending on your long-term purchasing goals, it may be important to consider a field's growth rates and the salary range you prefer. For example, the Bureau of Labor Statistics reports that civil engineering may have an 8% growth rate in the next nine years.

Connect with professionals. Consider asking an engineering professional some questions about their specialization, including about their work environment and day-to-day responsibilities. You can either ask a mentor you know personally or participate in a networking activity.

Take introductory courses. If you're already taking classes at a college or university, you may benefit from taking a course on a specialization's fundamentals. You can also bond with your professor and classmates, who may help you find career opportunities in the future.

Use school resources. Academic advisors or career centers can offer helpful advice and valuable insights when choosing a civil engineering specialization to study. For instance, they can provide an assessment tool or decision-making outline to help guide your process.

Review degree requirements. Reading the curriculums for each specialization to learn which classes you can take can help you determine what type of academic trajectory you prefer. Some specializations offer more coursework, while others involve independent projects or professional training opportunities.
